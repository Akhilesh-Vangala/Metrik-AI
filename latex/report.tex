\begin{abstract}
This report describes Metrik~AI, a Python pipeline that predicts hourly building energy consumption, flags anomalies in the predictions, and ranks buildings for operational audit. We work with the ASHRAE Great Energy Predictor~III dataset---20.2 million hourly meter readings from 1,449 buildings across 16 sites. The pipeline runs out-of-core with chunked I/O, builds 38 leakage-safe features, and trains a LightGBM model that cuts RMSE by 82\% relative to a per-meter mean baseline. A modified Z-score detector flags 44,363 anomalous hours (2.2\% of validation), and a composite scorer produces a ranked audit list of 964 building--meter pairs. We benchmark several acceleration techniques---NumPy vectorization, Cython, Numba, CuPy, and multiprocessing---and report measured speedups ranging from 1.5$\times$ (parallel training) to 36.5$\times$ (vectorized scoring) on real data.
\end{abstract}

% ============================================================================
\section{Introduction}
% ============================================================================

Buildings are responsible for roughly 40\% of total U.S.\ energy consumption, and a large portion of that energy is wasted~\cite{miller2020}. The root cause is straightforward: most commercial buildings still run heating, cooling, and lighting on fixed schedules rather than responding to actual demand. If a building operator had an accurate hourly forecast of energy use, they could pre-cool before a heatwave, catch a malfunctioning chiller before the monthly bill arrives, or figure out which of their 200 buildings to retrofit first.

That is the problem we tackle here. Metrik~AI takes hourly meter readings, weather, and building metadata, and produces three outputs: (1)~per-meter consumption forecasts, (2)~anomaly flags on the residuals, and (3)~a ranked audit list that tells an operator where to look first. This three-part structure mirrors the Measurement \& Verification (M\&V) workflow that energy services companies actually use in practice~\cite{molina2017}.

On the Python side, the project uses most of the techniques from DS-GA~1019 on a dataset large enough that they matter: chunked I/O, vectorized feature engineering, Cython and Numba for compiled kernels, GPU code via CuPy and Numba CUDA, process- and thread-based parallelism, PySpark, \texttt{scipy.optimize} for hyperparameter search, and profiling with \texttt{cProfile} and \texttt{tracemalloc}. Each one was applied to a real bottleneck we encountered while building the pipeline, not as an afterthought.

\section{Related Work}
% ============================================================================

\subsection{Building Energy Prediction}

Predicting building energy consumption has been studied extensively over the past decade, driven by both environmental regulation and the availability of smart-meter data. Runge and Zmeureanu~\cite{runge2019} provide a thorough review of neural-network-based approaches for building energy forecasting, noting that models need to handle strong daily and weekly seasonality, weather dependencies, and building-specific characteristics. Earlier work relied on physics-based simulation (e.g., EnergyPlus), but data-driven methods have largely overtaken them for operational use because they can adapt to individual buildings without requiring detailed thermal models~\cite{molina2017}.

Gradient boosted trees---LightGBM in particular~\cite{ke2017}---have become a popular choice in this domain. The top solutions in the ASHRAE Great Energy Predictor~III Kaggle competition (2019, 4,370 teams) almost universally used LightGBM or XGBoost, often with per-site or per-meter model ensembles. The key insight from those solutions is that lag features and rolling statistics carry the bulk of the predictive signal, while weather and building metadata provide useful but secondary context. Our approach follows this finding.

\subsection{Anomaly Detection in Energy Data}

Anomaly detection on building meter data typically falls into two camps: unsupervised statistical methods and model-based residual analysis. The modified Z-score, which uses the median and median absolute deviation (MAD) rather than the mean and standard deviation, is well suited for energy data because it is robust to the heavy-tailed distributions common in meter readings~\cite{iglewicz1993}. We use the residual-based variant: rather than flagging raw readings, we score the difference between actual and predicted consumption, which separates genuine anomalies from buildings that simply have high baseline usage.

\subsection{The ASHRAE GEPIII Dataset}

The ASHRAE Great Energy Predictor~III dataset was published as part of the Building Data Genome Project~2~\cite{miller2020}. It contains 20.2\,M hourly readings from 1,449 non-residential buildings across 16 sites in North America and Europe. The dataset is publicly available on Kaggle and has been used in over 100 subsequent papers. Known data quality issues (Site~0 kBTU units, long zero-reading streaks from meter outages, missing weather) are well documented, and we handle all of them in our cleaning pipeline.

\section{Data and Preprocessing}
% ============================================================================

The training set consists of 20.2\,M rows: hourly readings from 1,449 buildings with 4 meter types (electricity, chilled water, steam, hot water). Auxiliary tables provide building metadata (square footage, year built, primary use) and site-level hourly weather (temperature, humidity, wind, cloud coverage, precipitation).

The whole CSV is about 1.3\,GB. Rather than loading it in one shot (which pushes memory past 4\,GB after merging in metadata and weather), we stream it in chunks of 2\,M rows using \texttt{pandas.read\_csv(chunksize=...)} wrapped in a Python generator (\texttt{yield}). In dev mode, \texttt{itertools.islice} limits the number of chunks. Each chunk is merged with in-memory metadata and weather, then cleaned.

Three cleaning steps run per chunk. First, Site~0 electricity readings are converted from kBTU to kWh (multiply by 0.293071). Second, outliers are capped at the 99.9th percentile per building-meter pair. Third, consecutive zero-reading streaks $\geq$48\,h (likely meter outages) are replaced with NaN. Weather columns (10--40\% missing) are imputed per-site via forward-fill then backward-fill. Table~\ref{tab:data_quality} summarizes per-site quality after cleaning.

\section{Methodology}
% ============================================================================

\subsection{Feature Engineering}

We build 38 features in six groups: \textbf{temporal} (10)---hour, day-of-week, month, weekend flag, holiday flag via the \texttt{holidays} library cached with \texttt{@lru\_cache}, day-of-year, and sine/cosine encodings for hour and month; \textbf{lag} (3)---readings from 24\,h and 168\,h ago plus the 24\,h difference; \textbf{rolling} (8)---mean, std, min, max over 24\,h and 168\,h windows, shifted by one step to prevent leakage; \textbf{building} (3)---log square footage, building age, primary-use code; \textbf{weather} (8)---temperatures, humidity, wind chill, cloud coverage, wind speed; \textbf{interaction} (4)---hour$\times$weekend, temperature$\times$hour, sqft$\times$temp, reading-vs-rolling ratio.

Everything is stored as \texttt{float32} to halve memory. The final feature list is assembled from sub-lists using \texttt{itertools.chain}. We keep a deliberately slow ``naive'' version using \texttt{iterrows} to serve as a profiling baseline. Leakage safety is verified by unit tests that check no feature accesses future timestamps.

\subsection{Forecasting Models}

We use a time-based train/validation split (last 3 months held out, no shuffling) and train three models:

\begin{enumerate}[noitemsep,leftmargin=*]
\item \textbf{Baseline Mean}: per-building-meter historical average---the simplest possible predictor.
\item \textbf{Baseline Lag-24h}: yesterday's reading at the same hour. Surprisingly strong for buildings with stable daily patterns.
\item \textbf{LightGBM}~\cite{ke2017}: gradient boosted trees, 63 leaves, learning rate 0.05, early stopping after 50 rounds. Categorical features (primary use, site, meter type) are handled natively. We also use \texttt{scipy.optimize.minimize\_scalar} (bounded Brent's method) to search for the optimal learning rate in $[0.005, 0.3]$.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Anomaly Detection}

Residuals (actual $-$ predicted) are scored per building-meter group using the modified Z-score:
\begin{equation}
M_i = \frac{0.6745\,(x_i - \tilde{x})}{\text{MAD}}, \quad \text{MAD} = \text{median}(|x_i - \tilde{x}|)
\end{equation}
where $\tilde{x}$ is the group median. Hours with $|M_i| > 3.5$ are flagged. Consecutive flagged hours are clustered into temporal streaks---a 72-hour streak is a much bigger concern than three isolated spikes. Per-site severity summaries use \texttt{collections.defaultdict} and \texttt{itertools.accumulate}.

\subsection{Decision Support}

A composite priority score ranks each building-meter pair by anomaly rate, mean severity, total excess consumption (positive residuals summed), and longest streak. The top entries form the audit list---a ranked output telling operators which meters to inspect first.

\section{Optimization and Acceleration}
% ============================================================================

We used \texttt{cProfile} for CPU profiling and \texttt{tracemalloc} for peak memory to identify the actual bottlenecks before applying any optimization. All configuration dataclasses use \texttt{@dataclass(slots=True)} to avoid per-instance \texttt{\_\_dict\_\_} overhead, and \texttt{float32} dtypes halve memory relative to \texttt{float64} (15.3\,MB $\to$ 7.6\,MB for a 2\,M-row frame) with negligible precision loss.

\subsection{NumPy Vectorization}

The biggest win. Replacing a Python \texttt{for} loop over 1\,M residuals with \texttt{np.median} and array arithmetic drops runtime from 0.66\,s to 0.018\,s---36.5$\times$. Building 38 features on 50K rows takes 0.73\,s with \texttt{iterrows} and 0.14\,s vectorized (5.3$\times$). The lesson: before reaching for Cython or Numba, try vectorizing first.

\subsection{Cython}

We wrote three functions in \texttt{cython\_kernels.pyx}: modified Z-score, rolling mean, and rolling std. The code uses \texttt{cdef} declarations, \texttt{np.ndarray[np.float64\_t, ndim=1]} typed memoryviews, \texttt{cimport libc.math} for C-speed \texttt{fabs}/\texttt{sqrt}, and compiler directives \texttt{boundscheck(False)}, \texttt{wraparound(False)}, \texttt{cdivision(True)}. Built with a standard \texttt{setup.py} using \texttt{cythonize}. Result: 15.3$\times$ speedup at 1\,M rows. NumPy still beats it on anomaly scoring because \texttt{np.median} is already heavily optimized in C under the hood, but Cython wins on custom kernels without a clean NumPy one-liner.

\subsection{Numba JIT}

Five functions use \texttt{@njit(parallel=True, cache=True)} with \texttt{prange} for parallel loops. Numba scores 5.1$\times$ on anomaly detection and 3.5$\times$ on rolling mean vs.\ pandas. The numbers are lower than Cython partly because our benchmark only does one pass (JIT compilation overhead on the first call). In a production setting with cached compilation, the gap narrows.

We also wrote a Numba CUDA kernel (\texttt{@cuda.jit}) that assigns one thread per residual with blocks of 256, uses \texttt{cuda.grid(1)} for index computation, \texttt{cuda.to\_device()} for host-to-device transfer, and \texttt{.copy\_to\_host()} for retrieval. On machines without an NVIDIA GPU it falls back to the CPU path.

\subsection{CuPy GPU}

CuPy provides a NumPy-compatible API for GPU operations. Our rolling-mean GPU function uses \texttt{cp.cumsum} and \texttt{cp.where} to compute the sliding window in one pass without Python loops. An early version with a Python \texttt{for} loop over CuPy arrays was actually \textit{slower} than NumPy because every array access triggered a GPU--CPU sync---rewriting it as fully vectorized CuPy fixed the issue.

\subsection{Parallelism and Concurrency}

CPU-bound per-site model training uses \texttt{ProcessPoolExecutor} with \texttt{multiprocessing.get\_context("spawn")} (\texttt{fork} is unsafe on macOS and deprecated since Python 3.12). I/O-bound chunk reading uses \texttt{ThreadPoolExecutor}. We also built a \texttt{ThreadedResultCollector} class using the producer--consumer pattern: a \texttt{Queue} for dispatch, a \texttt{Lock} for shared state, and daemon \texttt{Thread} workers.

\subsection{PySpark}

A standalone Spark pipeline uses \texttt{SparkSession}, \texttt{Window} functions for lag/rolling features, and \texttt{RegressionEvaluator}. At 20\,M rows pandas is fine, but the Spark code is there for scaling to the full 53.6\,M.

\section{Software Engineering}

The codebase has 15 Python modules under \texttt{src/}, a Click CLI with 7 subcommands (\texttt{run}, \texttt{benchmark}, \texttt{parallel-benchmark}, \texttt{profile}, \texttt{spark}, \texttt{quality}, \texttt{eda}), YAML-based configuration parsed into typed dataclasses, and a Makefile wrapping all commands. We wrote 19 \texttt{pytest} tests covering leakage safety, Numba correctness against NumPy references, anomaly thresholds, temporal split properties, and an end-to-end integration test on synthetic data. Dependencies are pinned in \texttt{requirements.txt}, and the README documents every step from dataset download to execution.

% ============================================================================
\section{Conclusion and Future Work}
% ============================================================================

Metrik~AI shows that a Python codebase---without compiled languages or cloud infrastructure---can process millions of hourly energy readings, produce useful forecasts (82\% RMSE improvement), flag anomalies (2.2\% rate), and rank buildings for audit, all under two minutes on a laptop.

A few honest limitations. We ran on $\sim$7\,M rows rather than the full 20\,M for most experiments to keep iteration fast. The parallel speedup is modest because LightGBM already parallelizes internally. And we have no UI---output is CSVs and PNGs.

For future work: (1)~run on the full 53.6\,M rows with both training years; (2)~add a Streamlit dashboard for interactive exploration; (3)~try transfer learning across sites for buildings with limited history; (4)~deploy behind a REST API for real-time inference.
